Chapter One

9:14 P.M.

Maya Cole knew the exact moment the city died, because she felt it before she saw it. A drop in the hum. The particular silence of ten thousand air conditioners cutting out at once, like the whole island holding its breath.

Then Manhattan went black.

Not the rolling, apologetic dark of a summer brownout — block by block, borough by borough. This was instant. Total. From the forty-first floor she watched the grid she had spent four years of her life building wink out in a single, impossible heartbeat: Midtown, the financial district, the bridges, the rivers of headlights on the FDR all stuttering to a halt at the same frozen second.

"That's not possible," said Devlin behind her, and she almost laughed, because she'd written the report that proved exactly how possible it was. Eighteen months ago. They'd thanked her for her concern and walked her out of the building.

· · ·

Her phone buzzed once in the dark. One bar of battery, no signal — and a message that had no business arriving at all, from a number she'd deleted a long time ago.

She read it twice. Her hands were very steady, which was how she knew she was afraid.

You built a city that couldn't fall, it said. So I'm going to show you exactly how it does. You have ninety minutes. You always knew it would be you.

Below the words, a number had started to count down.

89:59.

89:58.

Maya looked out at eight million people who had no idea yet, at a darkness that went all the way to the water, and understood with perfect clarity that whatever happened next, the version of the world she had woken up in that morning was already gone.

She turned away from the window and started to run.
